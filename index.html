Grass Cutting Rover +

The aim of this project was to design, build and develop a grass cutting mobile robot that can traverse different terrains and environments and incorporate visual AI models to aid it in understanding its surroundings.

The rover prototype successfully passed preliminary testing where it was cutting fixed areas of grass, detect obstacles in its path, deploy live footage inference and attract interest from early-stage investors.

CAD & 3D Printing Electronic Circuitry Visual AI Application

The final aim of the project was to produce a modular robot that can handle multiple outdoor tasks (grass cutting, leaf collection, pesticide spraying, etc) through conveniently switching between the tools required. It was meant to have a robust main frame which the tool kits can then be attached to.

Then equipped with dual frontal cameras and usage of cutting-edge visual AI models, the robot can then understand its surrounding and behave accordingly (obstacle avoidance).

Hardware

  • Chassis: Uses aluminium frames for stability and 3D printed plates cover to reduce weight and segment the inner chambers of the chassis.
  • Wheels: 4-wheels using high-torque DC motors whose direction is controlled use 4 servo motors.
  • Suspension System: Uses the rocker-bogie suspension system (often found in NASA Rovers) to handle uneven terrain while keeping the chassis level.
  • Cutting Disc: Adjustable three blades grass cutting disc attached to the bottom of the rover. The height of grass cutting can be controlled.
  • Computing & Control: The rover is equipped with a Jetson Orin Nano to handle most of the control code and running inference of AI models, and an Arduino Mega used for low-level control of the motor drivers and sensors.
  • Sensors: IMU Sensor for orientation, dual frontal cameras for live footages.

Software

  • FreeCAD: The CAD software used to design all of the 3D printed parts such as the chassis and the motor housings.
  • AI Models: DepthAnything V2 was used to acquire accurate and rapid depth map of the front of the rover. META AI – FastSAM was also used to acquire image segmentation and object identification of the scene.
  • Arduino: Low level control of the motors and communication with a python script to handle control.

Development Process

  • Phase 1: Mechanical and electronic design of the rover went through various iterations of building then testing before settling for the final prototype design.
  • Phase 2: Mainly the software aspects of the rover regarding smooth motor control, communication between sensors and between the Arduino and the Jetson. Running inference on live visual footages was also in this stage.

    • The first stage of the project was to settle on the physical design of the rover and integrate the low-level controls. From here, development would have expanded more on the software to included more robust navigation (through VSLAM), autonomy and optimization of the AI Models run-time.

Quadcopter Drone +

A personal project that I initiated due to gaining new found interest in drones and aviation robotics. I built and assembled the drone from scratch rather than buying a prebuilt one as to explore the intricacy of drone design. Learned a lot regarding drone electronics, aerial control and autonomy through software simulations.

Drone Electronics Simulation and Autonomy

Research was first done on the critical components involved with a drone, then choice of electronics was made based on flight time and size of the drone. The drone frame was made out of carbon fibre, electronics were soldered (flight controller, motors, GPS, ESC and power distribution) before finally designing and 3D printing some blade and battery guards.

While I did find interest in drones, I never actually flew one. Therefore, before attempting any flights, I practiced on a drone simulator called Microsoft’s AirSim. This simulation was later on expanded on not to just have manual control of the drone, but also attempt running semi-autonomous missions by having AirSim be connected to ArduPilot (The flight controller software used) before real life deployment.

A single board computer, KHADAS, was attached to the drone to conduct simple live flights and run DepthAnything model for depth imaging from a monocular camera allowing the drone to detect obstacles and halt.

Hardware Components

  • Brushless DC Motors
  • SpeedyBee F405 Flight Controller and ESC Stack
  • Matek GPS Module
  • Simple Monocular Camera
  • Khadas VIM3 Single Board Computer

Software

  • ArduPilot Flight Controller: An open-sources autopilot system for many vehicles such as copters, fixed wing aircraft and rovers.
  • Microsoft AirSIM: A simulation platform for AI research and experimentation for aerial mobility and autonomous systems. It was used in conjunction with ArduPilot to simulate autonomous drone missions.

Final Year Project (FYP) and Thesis +

The title I chose for my FYP was “The Design and Development of a Teleoperated Anthropomorphic Hand” as I was interested in combining human hand anatomy and robotics by creating a robotic hand (limited to fingers) that would then mimic the operator hand movement in real time where the operator hand is the master manipulator and the animatronic hand is the slave manipulator.

SolidWorks Flex Sensors Anthropomorphic

The teleoperated anthropomorphic hand system consists of the several major parts. The sensory glove (master manipulator) collects the user finger and wrist positions using flex pressure sensors and sends it to the Arduino microcontroller.

The Arduino microcontroller communicates with the PC in order to resolve the kinematics needed and send the results for the final position to the animatronic hand (slave manipulator). The animatronic hand, having 16 DoF, was 3D printed and fitted with servomotors connected to mechanical links which would then move to the targe position.

Evaluation of the teleoperation system and the animatronic hand is done through experimental tasks to check the hand gripping capabilities as well as the range and accuracy of motion.

Hardware

  • 3D printed fingers and anthropomorphic hand
  • Flex sensors that measure the amount of deflection/bending of the user fingers
  • Potentiometer for measuring of user wrist bending
  • Servo motors acting as the fingers’ actuators

Software

  • CAD: SolidWorks was used to 3D design the hand and fingers and simulate their movement.
  • Arduino: C programming, Arduino was used to control the animatronic hand and facilitate communication between the operator sensory glove and the robotic hand.
